How to present Prague

Prague is an old and charming city. It has more than a million inhabitants, it lies on the meandres of the river Vltava, its roofs are red and streets are narrow, and there are thousands of random tales and great deeds stored in its rich historical possesion. So for instance, did you know that there was a wall built by hungry people, called the Hungry wall, built only to provide work for the starving and unemployed, so they had money for living? And did you know we have a history of throwing our councilors out of the windows of the Town hall, and that we have done this three times in only two hundreds years?

Prague is an old city and if you want to fully engage in its beauty, you need to lose yourself in its large squares and narrow streets. If I were to recommend places that you must see, I would do it geographically because there is nothing more magical to me in Prague, than the fact that you can consequently walk through all its history.

The Old Town

Whether you're an antiquity lover or an admirer of the modern times, start by roaming the Old Town, and you will have no regrets. The best way to get there is by your feet. There is a building of great cultural value in Prague, standing in the front of a kilometer long Wenceslaw square. It's the National Museum, wihtin its walls is stored history, and outside its walls, history happens. The Wenceslaw square hosted many significant demonstrations throughout the centuries, as well as in the recent times, and with its symbolical value, it lies on the edge of culture, history and modern city life.

You can walk down the Wenceslaw square, turn left and encounter another significant street, Národní třída, at the end of which stands the majestic National theater with its golden roof and heavy wide stone blocks. Before you reach this iconic building, though, your attention will be drawn to the new scene of the Nation theatre, distinctive architectonal piece of brutalism.

Taking a few steps back, you can decide to turn the other way, already back at the top of the Wenceslaw square. If you turned your head to the right, you would see a building in distance, and if you walk one of the streets on the side, eventually, you reach the Prague main train station. To see its tall and gentle iron constructions leaning above the rileways, you would have to go inside and find your way to the platforms. Perhaps, explore the insides if you decide to explore another czech city. When you pass the train station building and walk through a parc, you encounter a tram station. If you go turn to the left and proceed walking, another train station appears in front of you, but this time you only need to peek in to spot the atmosphere and the trails.

Unless you're a train lover, you leave both stations behind, and continue walking between two rows of tenement houses. Very soon you need choose direction again, and left side it is. Another complex square opens in fron of you, Náměstí Republiky, the Republic square. Several buildings may catch your attention. There is a national bank, a famous theatre, two unique shopping malls and tramway trails cutting t center of the paved street without sidewalks. There is a tower, the Powder tower, another remarcable monument you might have heard about. If you walk beneath it, you find yourself in Celetná street, which will lead you to the Old Town Square, the one, you have most likely come to see.

The Old Town square is the place where Prague people throw others out of windows. The Towns hall, large temple and other significant building may catch your eye, just as you walk into the large space of the square. The most famous piece of work is the astronomical clock, called Orloj, bound by legends about poor clocksman who was blinded by spiteful and avaricious town councilours. The clock moves nicely every full hour, so make sure to tackle your timing to not miss the show.

Once you're full of Old Town square, you can walk past a Nicolas Church to enter another square of Jan Palach, where the orchestral building Rudolfinum stands. Concerts are taking place there, official ceremonies, too. There is a bridge seemingly leading you closer to the Prague castle, but for the real experience, I recommend taking another rout. For deepre experience you can roam a bit the streets there, to see the new towns hall, the city library, and finally also the Nation library inside of a large and complex building called Clementinum, where jezuits used to have their schools and dorms, before it was publically redone to its current form. If you follow the wall of the gigant building, you get near to DAMA, theatre faculty of the academy of fine arts, then you pass the Salvator church and right in front of you rises a tall Prague tower and the Charles bridge behind it. Did you know that the date and time it was established at, gives the row of prime numbers from 1 to 9, and back to 1? 1357, 9. 7. 5:31 in the morning.

The Lesser Town

You have just walked the Charles bridge, admired Prague caste, all the spires and towers in the distance, the golden roof of the National theater, the river beneath you and its wide bed, and now, you stare at the gigant Prague caste, probably under the impression that the caste itself consists of on the two large spires that you see up there, which, in fact, is are only the towers of Saint Vit cathedral that stands inside of the castle's walls. You have climb a lot of stairs to get up there, but the view is worth every one of them. After you've seen all of the castle's squares (it has many) and the cathedral itself, you take a while to look around from one of the many viewpoints, and try to spot the buildings that you have already met, andd tips of churches that you passed. Churches and towers are great for orientation.

You come back down from the castle and explore how the atmosphere of the Lesser Town differs. There is a hill called Petřín, where the aforementioned Hungry wall is to be seen, and mainly, there's a historic cableway that takes you up the hill, where you can visit an astronomy building, famous just as well.
